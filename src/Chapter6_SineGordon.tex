    \section{The Sine Gordon Equation}\label{sec:the-sine-gordon-equation}
    Sine Gordon is good for wave fronts.

    \[
        u_{tt} - u_{xx} = \sin(u) = 0
    \]

    We will look at solutions to the traveling wave equation using the ansatz below:
    \[
        u(x,t) = f(x-ct)
    \]
    Let's take some derivatives:
    \begin{align*}
        u_{t} =  -c f'(x-ct) &\quad u_{tt} = c^2 f''(x-ct) \\
        u_{x} = f'(x-ct) &\quad u_{xx} = f''(x-ct)
    \end{align*}
    Now we can substitute into $u_{tt} - u_{xx} + \sin(u)$:
    \[
        c^2 f'' - f'' + \sin(f)=0
    \]
    We can form a nicer equation by multiplying by $f'$
    \begin{align*}
        f'(c^2 f'' - f'' + \sin(f))&=0\\
        c^2 f'' f' - f'' f' + \sin(f)f'&=0\\
        f'' f' (c^2 - 1) + \sin(f)f'&=0
    \end{align*}
    We can now integrate to produce a first order equation
    \[
        \frac{1}{2} (c^2 -1) (f')^2 - \cos(f) = a
    \]
    with $a$ as some constant of integration.
    $a$ can be different depending on the problem.
    To determine $a$, we apply the decay conditions for a traveling wave front.
    As the wave extends toward infinity ($y \to \infty$), the wave profile must flatten out.
    Therefore, we assume the decay conditions:
    \[
        \lim_{y\to\infty} f(y) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{y\to\infty} f'(y) = 0
    \]
    Taking the limit of our integrated equation as $y \to \infty$:
    \begin{align*}
        a &= \lim_{y\to\infty} \left[ \frac{1}{2}(c^2-1)(f'(y))^2 - \cos(f(y)) \right] \\
        a &= 0 - \cos(0) \\
        a &= -1
    \end{align*}
    Substituting $a = -1$ back into our equation allows us to separate variables and solve for the wave front (kink) solution.

    Substituting $a = -1$ back into our first-order equation gives:
    \[
        \frac{1}{2} (c^2 -1) (f')^2 - \cos(f) = -1
    \]

    We can rearrange this equation to solve for $(f')^2$:
    \[
        (f')^2 = 2\left(\frac{1 - \cos(f)}{1 - c^2}\right)
    \]

    Taking the square root of both sides gives us a separable differential equation:
    \[
        f' = \pm \sqrt{\frac{2}{1 - c^2}} \sqrt{1 - \cos(f)}
    \]

    Now, we separate the variables so that all terms with $f$ are on one side and terms with $x$ are on the other:
    \[
        \frac{df}{\sqrt{1 - \cos(f)}} = \pm \sqrt{\frac{2}{1 - c^2}} dx
    \]

    You are smart and can figure out the rest using partial fractions and stuff.
